Aid worker diary: “My generation has never experienced a crisis of such scale”

29 Jan 2015

 
16 Jan 2015, Kharkov, Ukraine: Marina, from Donetsk, gave birth to her daughter in Kharkov, after she had fled her home and left behind her family. "I have my reasons to smile: my daughter and the fact that some relatives are still alive," she says. Credit: OCHA/Z. Nurmukhambetova

Anna, a native of the war-torn city of Donetsk, recently joined OCHA’s team in Kharkov, eastern Ukraine. Anna reflects on her shift from someone affected by the crisis, to someone involved in the humanitarian response.

Update: since this story was written, violence in Donetsk has intensified, forcing more people to leave the city and head for Kharkov and other points of refuge. Volunteers in Kharkov say that hundreds of people are ready to leave the conflict zone once secure evacuation routes are open.

It took me eight hours on a bus, standing theentire trip, to make it to Kharkov from Donetsk.

I am at “Station Kharkov”, a locally run aid centre where displaced people can get food, clothes, medicines, psychological assistance and even temporary housing and jobs.

This place was founded by Eva, a soft-spoken IT freelancer in her late twenties. In less than a year, Eva has built a volunteer empire that has helped thousands of displaced people in the city receive aid.

When the crisis started, Eva decided to volunteer on a government run helpline.

“As an emergency hotline volunteer, I could see that our authorities were overwhelmed and could not respond to all of the needs of the displaced people,” Eva explains to me.

The city of Kharkov and the wider Kharkov region host the highest number of people fleeing violence in eastern Ukraine. As of 21 January, up to 120,000 people have come to Kharkov region. And as new violence erupts in and around Donetsk, “Station Kharkov” and the rest of us brace for a new wave of displaced people.

“I am in awe of what people like Eva are doing”

Over 1 million people have so far been affected by the crisis. My generation has never experienced a crisis of such scale. I am in awe of what people like Eva are doing.

Eva takes me to what they call an adaptation centre for displaced people. It is a place for people stay before they can find an apartment or something more permanent. Small rooms, common facilities, and common kitchen. It makes me think of my parents back in Donetsk and the risks they are taking to remain in our apartment. This is the kind of life my parents are avoiding. But this kind of life has given relief to many people who fled constant shelling and uncertainty.

Five-month old Zlata sleeps in the common area at the adaptation centre. Our conversations, cartoons blasting on TV, different volunteers coming and going – nothing interrupts the baby’s peaceful sleep. “She is used to all the noise around her,” sighs Zlata’s mother Marina who was six-months pregnant when she left Donetsk.

Everything will be alright!

At the train station in Kharkov, Eva introduces me to Alla Feshenko, a volunteer who meets and greets new arrivals from the conflict areas. Alla’s face is a mix of exhaustion and hope. Amid information bulletins and candies for the incoming refugees are signs in Russian that read “Everything will be alright!” For a moment, I feel hopeful.

“People used to come here thinking they won’t stay long,” says Alla, “But recently the mood changed, and now people come with a feeling it is their final destination.”  It is a sobering observation that breaks my heart.

I finish my time with Station Kharkov volunteers at a distribution centre where crowds of people have gathered to receive heaters and food. This is what they need most, as well as housing, clothing and warm blankets.

I cannot keep myself from feeling proud when I see the United Nations support to Station Kharkov. Kids leave the aid centre with new UNICEF-equipped backpacks; parents carry food donated by UNDP or heaters provided by the Yuriy Sapronov Fund.

So much more needs to be done for these people and others still trapped in the zone of conflict. I realize that I need to be part of this big humanitarian family just as much as my people need help.
